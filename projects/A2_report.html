Overall Analysis Questions

  1. Which municipalities have the most restrictive zoning policies based on the ratio of single-family housing to total housing?

    2. In Exclusionary by Design, historical accounts show that certain suburbs deliberately expanded single-family zoning to shield themselves from demographic changes and higher-density developments. By pinpointing which places still carry the highest ratio of SF zoning today, we can examine whether these towns have maintained longstanding restrictive practices that limit housing diversity, effectively mirroring past policies even in a contemporary context.

  2. How does the percentage of single-family zoning correlate with median household income?

    3. Drawing on the insights from The Illegal City of Somerville, which highlights how local codes can deviate from real housing needs, a strong link between SF zoning and higher incomes might reveal a subtle mechanism of economic exclusion. While municipalities often frame single-family regulations as preserving “neighborhood character,” real-world evidence could indicate that these rules inadvertently (or intentionally) segregate communities by income, perpetuating wealth disparities initially rooted in older zoning strategies.

  3. How does single-family zoning relate to racial demographics—do municipalities with more SF zoning have higher percentages of white residents?

    4. Exclusionary by Design notes how large-lot mandates frequently served to deter non-white and lower-income households. Investigating whether places with intense single-family restrictions still host predominantly white populations can clarify if modern zoning quietly sustains racially homogeneous enclaves. Such findings would shed light on whether past exclusionary frameworks have simply evolved into a new guise—namely, the “race-neutral” language of protecting property values and local aesthetic traditions.

Discoveries & Insights

Summary

Taken together, the charts reveal a consistent theme: municipalities with higher proportions of single-family zoning tend to show higher median incomes, fewer total households (due to lower density), and a modestly greater share of white residents. By contrast, multi-family or non-single-family areas exhibit more varied household sizes—particularly single-person households—along with slightly broader racial diversity. Although differences in age are relatively small between these two housing types, the correlation between single-family exclusivity and higher incomes is pronounced, suggesting that zoning rules play a central role in shaping local demographics and economic barriers to entry.
